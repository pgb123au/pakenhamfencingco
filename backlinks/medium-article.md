# Fencing a Home in the Pakenham Growth Corridor: What Cardinia's Soil, Wind and the Fences Act Actually Mean for Your Boundary

Drive through Cardinia Lakes, Lakeside, Heritage Springs or the newer pockets of Officer on any weekend and you'll see the same scene repeated lot after lot: freshly handed-over house blocks, bare boundaries, and homeowners trying to work out who is responsible for the fence between them. Pakenham and the wider Cardinia Shire have been one of Melbourne's fastest-growing corridors for the better part of two decades, and that growth has created a very particular fencing challenge that most new owners never see coming.

This is a plain-English guide to getting fencing right the first time in this corridor — the soil, the wind, the pool-safety traps, the neighbour rules, and what it actually costs in 2026.

## Why the corridor is a special case

Two things make fencing here different from an established inner-suburb block.

First, the **estates built in the early-to-mid 2000s are now ageing out of their first fences**. The timber paling fences put up when Cardinia Lakes and Heritage Springs were first released are now 18 to 20-plus years old. Treated-pine palings and posts sitting in reactive clay simply don't last forever, and a whole wave of those original boundaries is failing at once — leaning lines, greyed and split palings, and posts rotted through at the ground line. If your fence went up with the house and the house is around two decades old, you are squarely in replacement territory.

Second, the **brand-new lots in Lakeside and the latest Officer releases are badly exposed**. There are few mature trees and few neighbouring structures to break the wind, so boundary fences cop the full force of the gusts that funnel across the flat corridor. A fence that would stand fine in a sheltered established street needs more thought here — post spacing, footing depth and panel choice all matter more on an open new-estate block.

## The soil under your feet

Across the Casey-Cardinia plain the ground is dominated by **reactive clay**. It swells when wet and shrinks when dry, and that constant seasonal movement is brutal on anything anchored shallowly. For fencing it means one thing above all: **post footings have to be deep enough and the right diameter** for the soil and the fence height, or the line will start leaning within a couple of seasons. This is the single most common reason a fence fails early in the corridor — not the panels, the posts shifting in the clay. A contractor who works the area every week will footing the posts properly rather than to the bare minimum, and on the worst sites will concrete them deeper than you'd expect.

## The rules you actually have to follow

A handful of codes and laws govern fencing here. You don't need to memorise them, but you should know they exist before you order anything:

- **Fences Act 1968 (Vic)** — the law covering who pays for a shared boundary (dividing) fence. As a rule, adjoining owners contribute equally to a "sufficient dividing fence." If you want something fancier than sufficient, you generally cover the difference. Before you build or replace a boundary fence, you serve your neighbour a **Fencing Notice** setting out what you propose and the estimated cost. Getting this one step right avoids the single most common neighbour dispute in new estates — the argument that starts after the fence is already up.
- **Cardinia Shire fencing local law and permits** — the council sets local requirements, including provisions around front fences and **corner-lot sightlines** (the splay that keeps driveway and intersection visibility clear). Many estates in the corridor also carry a **developer's design covenant** restricting front-fence height, materials and colour. Always check your covenant before ordering, because it overrides your personal preference — and the developer can make you pull a non-compliant fence down.
- **Victorian pool and spa barrier regulations (Building Regulations 2018) plus AS 1926.1** — if you have or are putting in a pool or spa, the barrier rules are non-negotiable. Minimum 1.2 m effective barrier height, a gate that is self-closing and self-latching and opens outward, latch-height requirements, and a strict **non-climbable zone** (the 900 mm NCZ) that must be kept clear of anything a child could use to climb. In Victoria pool and spa barriers must be registered with the council and inspected — and Cardinia enforces it.
- **AS 1725 — chain-link fencing** — the relevant standard for rural and security chain-mesh boundaries.

For rural-residential and acreage blocks on the fringes of the shire — out toward Beaconsfield and Pakenham Upper — **bushfire-prone overlays** can also dictate compliant materials near the dwelling. It's worth pulling your planning property report before you commit to a fence type.

## What it costs in 2026 (and a worked example)

Honest, current ballpark rates for the corridor, supplied and installed:

- **Colorbond**: roughly **$90–$130 per lineal metre** — the corridor standard for durability and low maintenance
- **Timber paling**: roughly **$80–$120 per lineal metre**
- **Glass and aluminium pool fencing**: roughly **$300–$600 per lineal metre** (frameless glass sits at the top)
- **Rural post-and-rail / stock fencing**: roughly **$40–$80 per lineal metre**

Where you land in each range depends on height, ground conditions, removal of the old fence, and site access.

**Worked example — a typical Cardinia Lakes replacement.** Say you're replacing a 20-year-old timber boundary on a standard estate block: around 30 lineal metres along the back, plus 12 metres down one side that you share with a neighbour. You choose 1.8 m Colorbond.

- 42 lin m of Colorbond at, say, $110/lin m = **$4,620**
- Removal and tip fees for the old timber, allow **$600–$900**

So roughly **$5,200–$5,500 all up**. Now the Fences Act part: the 12 m shared run is a dividing fence, so — assuming your neighbour agrees to a standard Colorbond fence after you serve a proper Fencing Notice — they contribute to half of that section. That's a meaningful saving, which is exactly why serving the notice properly matters *before* the work starts, not after.

## The practical checklist

1. Find your title covenant and check front-fence height, material and colour restrictions before you choose anything.
2. If it's a shared boundary, serve a Fencing Notice and agree the spec with your neighbour in writing.
3. Insist on proper post-footing depth for reactive clay — this is what decides whether your fence is still straight in five years.
4. If there's a pool or spa, treat the barrier rules and AS 1926.1 as the law they are — 1.2 m height, the self-latching gate, and a clear 900 mm non-climbable zone.
5. Register and have the pool barrier inspected where required, and check corner-splay rules on corner lots.
